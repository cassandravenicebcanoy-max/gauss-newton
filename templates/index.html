Nonlinear Least Squares

Iterative curve fitting for nonlinear models via linearized normal equations

Mathematical Foundation

Theory & Discussion

Problem Statement

Given $m$ observed data pairs $\{(x_i, y_i)\}_{i=1}^m$ and a nonlinear model $f(x;\,\mathbf{p})$ parametrized by $\mathbf{p} = (p_1, p_2, \ldots, p_n)^T$, the goal is to minimize the residual sum of squares:

$$S(\mathbf{p}) = \sum_{i=1}^{m} r_i^2 = \|\mathbf{r}\|^2$$

where the residuals are defined as

$$r_i(\mathbf{p}) = y_i - f(x_i;\,\mathbf{p})$$

The Gauss–Newton Update

Starting from an initial guess $\mathbf{p}^{(0)}$, the method linearizes the model at each iterate using a first-order Taylor expansion:

$$f(x_i;\,\mathbf{p}+\boldsymbol{\delta}) \approx f(x_i;\,\mathbf{p}) + \mathbf{J}_i\,\boldsymbol{\delta}$$

where $\mathbf{J}$ is the Jacobian matrix:

$$J_{ij} = \frac{\partial f(x_i;\,\mathbf{p})}{\partial p_j}$$

Substituting into $S$ and minimizing over $\boldsymbol{\delta}$ yields the normal equations:

$$(\mathbf{J}^T\mathbf{J})\,\boldsymbol{\delta} = \mathbf{J}^T\mathbf{r}$$

The parameters are then updated:

$$\mathbf{p}^{(k+1)} = \mathbf{p}^{(k)} + \boldsymbol{\delta}^{(k)}$$

Algorithm Steps

  1. Choose initial parameter guess $\mathbf{p}^{(0)}$
  2. Compute residuals $\mathbf{r} = \mathbf{y} - \mathbf{f}(\mathbf{p})$
  3. Build the Jacobian $\mathbf{J}$ (analytically or via finite differences)
  4. Solve the normal equations $(\mathbf{J}^T\mathbf{J})\,\boldsymbol{\delta} = \mathbf{J}^T\mathbf{r}$
  5. Update: $\mathbf{p} \leftarrow \mathbf{p} + \boldsymbol{\delta}$
  6. Repeat until $\|\boldsymbol{\delta}\| < \varepsilon$ or max iterations reached

Convergence & Limitations

✓ Strengths

  • Quadratic convergence near the solution
  • No need for second derivatives (Hessian approximated as $\mathbf{J}^T\mathbf{J}$)
  • Works well when residuals are small at the solution

✗ Limitations

  • May diverge with poor initial guess
  • Singular $\mathbf{J}^T\mathbf{J}$ if parameters are linearly dependent
  • Slower on large-residual problems (Levenberg–Marquardt preferred)

Initial guess: $\mathbf{p}^{(0)} = (a_0, a_1) = (1.0,\,-1.0)$

Iteration 1 — Compute Residuals

Evaluate $f(x_i;\,1.0,\,-1.0) = e^{-x_i}$:

$$r_1 = 2.00 - 1.000 = 1.000$$ $$r_2 = 0.74 - 0.368 = 0.372$$ $$r_3 = 0.27 - 0.135 = 0.135$$ $$r_4 = 0.10 - 0.050 = 0.050$$

$S = 1.000^2 + 0.372^2 + 0.135^2 + 0.050^2 = 1.156$

Iteration 1 — Build Jacobian

Partial derivatives:

$$\frac{\partial f}{\partial a_0} = e^{a_1 x}, \qquad \frac{\partial f}{\partial a_1} = a_0\,x\,e^{a_1 x}$$ $$\mathbf{J} = \begin{pmatrix} e^{0} & 0 \\ e^{-1} & -e^{-1} \\ e^{-2} & -2e^{-2} \\ e^{-3} & -3e^{-3} \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 1.000 & 0.000 \\ 0.368 & -0.368 \\ 0.135 & -0.271 \\ 0.050 & -0.149 \end{pmatrix}$$
Iteration 1 — Normal Equations
$$\mathbf{J}^T\mathbf{J} = \begin{pmatrix} 1.289 & -0.218 \\ -0.218 & 0.215 \end{pmatrix}$$ $$\mathbf{J}^T\mathbf{r} = \begin{pmatrix} 1.185 \\ -0.156 \end{pmatrix}$$

Solving: $\boldsymbol{\delta}^{(1)} = (1.014,\, 0.534)^T$

Update: $\mathbf{p}^{(1)} = (1.0+1.014,\; -1.0+0.534) = (2.014,\,-0.466)$

After Convergence (≈ 6 iterations)

The algorithm converges to the optimal parameters:

$$a_0^* \approx 2.009, \qquad a_1^* \approx -1.002$$

This closely matches the true exponential $f(x) \approx 2\,e^{-x}$ with a final residual sum of squares $S^* \approx 0.0004$.

Result: $f(x) = 2.009\,e^{-1.002\,x}$
